# Diagnostic Question Banks

Per-dimension structured questions for user interviews.
Use these when the user has insider knowledge (employee, investor, board member) that public research can't capture.

---

## How to Use

Present questions in batches of 5-7. Tell the user:
> "You can answer in shorthand, skip anything too sensitive, or just say 'not sure' — even the shape of your uncertainty is useful data."

Mark each answer as:
- `[DIRECT]` — user directly observed this
- `[INFERRED]` — user's interpretation
- `[HEARD]` — secondhand, treat as signal not fact

---

## Dimension 1: Boundary Topology

### Hard Constraint Questions
1. What regulatory or legal constraints does the organization operate under that leadership treats as genuinely non-negotiable?
2. Have there been any recent moments where leadership felt a hard constraint was getting close to being breached? What was the reaction?
3. Are there any hard constraints that leadership treats as negotiable that probably shouldn't be?

### Soft Constraint Questions
4. What unwritten rules govern behavior here — things that would get you in trouble even if they're not in any policy document?
5. Has the organization's culture changed significantly in the last 3 years? In what direction?
6. How much do people trust leadership's word when it's not backed by a contract? Has that changed?

### Transaction Cost Questions
7. How hard is it to get things done across team or department boundaries? Is this getting easier or harder?
8. Where does internal bureaucracy or politics consume the most energy? What's the real cost of that?
9. Are there external partnerships or vendor relationships that have become dysfunctionally expensive to maintain or exit?

---

## Dimension 2: Incentive Architecture

### Incentive-Compatibility Questions
1. What behaviors does the organization say it values most? Now — what behaviors does it actually reward most visibly?
2. Can you name a person who was clearly doing the right thing for the organization but got passed over or punished for it?
3. Can you name a person who was clearly gaming the system and got rewarded for it?

### Principal-Agent Questions
4. How many layers of management sit between the person making a key decision and the people executing it? Does signal get lost?
5. Do front-line employees feel like the incentives they face align with what the organization actually needs from them?
6. Do managers optimize for what's good for the organization or for what makes their own performance look good? What's the difference?

### Game Dynamics Questions
7. Is competition within the organization mostly productive (raises the game) or destructive (people withhold information, sabotage others)?
8. Are there "commons" that people exploit rather than steward — shared resources, shared reputation, shared budget pools?
9. What does the compensation structure reward — individual performance, team performance, or organizational performance? How are they in tension?

---

## Dimension 3: Information Neurology

### Signal Fidelity Questions
1. When bad news happens, how quickly does it reach the people who need to act on it? Give a recent example.
2. Does leadership get the unfiltered truth? Or does information get softened, filtered, or delayed as it moves up?
3. Is there a "messenger gets shot" dynamic — people who bring bad news get penalized?

### Feedback Loop Questions
4. When something goes wrong, does the organization have a reliable mechanism for figuring out why and preventing recurrence? Or does it blame and move on?
5. Are there any problems that everyone knows about but that never seem to get addressed — "known unknowns" that the system can't fix?
6. Is there a mechanism for junior employees to challenge a bad decision made by senior leadership? Does it actually get used?

### Variety & Complexity Questions
7. Is the organization's decision-making process complex enough to handle the complexity of its environment? Or is it trying to simplify something that shouldn't be simplified?
8. Who are the people who see reality most clearly in this organization? Are they in the room when important decisions are made?
9. What do outsiders (customers, partners, competitors) know about this organization that insiders are slow to accept?

---

## Dimension 4: Temporal Metabolism

### Investment vs. Harvest Questions
1. Is the organization currently investing in its future capabilities or harvesting its existing ones? Which direction is the trend?
2. What would leadership sacrifice short-term performance for? What wouldn't they sacrifice it for?
3. Are there capabilities or relationships that are being slowly depleted that will be hard to rebuild?

### Optionality Questions
4. What strategic options does the organization currently have that it didn't have 3 years ago? What options has it foreclosed?
5. Is the organization over-committed to any single bet — technology, geography, customer segment, business model — that can't easily be changed?
6. How does leadership think about uncertainty — do they try to predict the future and bet on it, or maintain flexibility to respond to multiple futures?

### Renewal Questions
7. How does the organization bring in fresh thinking? Is it mostly promoted from within or does it bring in outside perspectives?
8. Are there processes, products, or structures that were useful 5 years ago but are now dead weight? Is leadership willing to kill them?
9. What would it take for this organization to fundamentally reinvent itself? Is that capacity present?

---

## Dimension 5: Legitimacy & Narrative

### Internal Narrative Questions
1. What story does the organization tell about why it exists and why it matters? Do employees believe it?
2. Is there cynicism about the organization's stated mission or values? How widespread? Among what groups?
3. When the organization does something that contradicts its stated values, how does it explain it internally?

### External Legitimacy Questions
4. Do key external stakeholders (customers, regulators, partners, community) still grant this organization legitimacy? Has that changed?
5. Are there any legitimacy debts accumulating — promises made that haven't been kept, trust extended that hasn't been honored?
6. How would the organization's story look from the outside if all internal communications were made public?

### Narrative Coherence Questions
7. Do different parts of the organization tell different stories about who they are? Is there narrative fragmentation?
8. Has a major event (scandal, crisis, leadership change, acquisition) disrupted the organization's sense of identity? Has it recovered?
9. What's the gap between the organization's best public narrative and its actual daily reality?

---

## Dimension 6: Coupling Architecture

### Internal Coupling Questions
1. If a major problem occurs in one part of the organization, how contained is it? Or does it ripple everywhere?
2. Are there single points of failure — key people, key systems, key relationships — whose loss would be catastrophic?
3. Has the organization ever experienced a cascade failure — where one problem triggered another and another? What was learned?

### External Dependency Questions
4. Which external dependencies (suppliers, platforms, regulators, partners) does the organization have no real alternative to?
5. Has any dependency that seemed safe turned out to be a vulnerability in the last 3 years?
6. How concentrated is revenue/customer/geography exposure? What would a shock to the most concentrated area look like?

### Buffer Questions
7. What reserves does the organization maintain — financial, talent, time, goodwill — to absorb unexpected shocks?
8. Has the organization been optimizing for efficiency at the expense of slack? Where does that feel dangerous?
9. How quickly can the organization change direction if it needs to? What are the biggest sources of inertia?

## Cross-Cutting Questions (Ask for any system)

These questions often reveal the most critical insights:

1. **The unspeakable**: What's the thing people in the organization know is true but feel they can't say out loud?
2. **The sacred cow**: What's the thing leadership treats as untouchable that probably needs to be questioned?
3. **The bright spot**: Where is the organization genuinely excellent in ways that might not be obvious from outside?
4. **The ticking clock**: What problem is accumulating that will force a crisis within 1-2 years if nothing changes?
5. **The exit question**: What would cause key people to leave? How close are they to that threshold?
6. **The counterfactual**: If this organization didn't exist, what would replace it, and would that be better or worse?
7. **The informed outsider**: What would a smart, well-informed person who just joined this organization notice in their first 90 days that veterans have stopped seeing?

---

## Question Selection Guide

| Situation | Priority questions |
|-----------|-------------------|
| User is insider (employee, exec) | All 7 cross-cutting + dimensions most relevant to presenting problem |
| User is investor/board member | D2 (incentives), D4 (temporal), D3 (information) + cross-cutting |
| User is competitor/market analyst | D1 (boundary), D5 (legitimacy), D6 (coupling) + public research |
| User has no insider access | Skip interview questions; rely on research protocol + visible signals |
| Crisis/urgent situation | Ticking clock + D3 + D6 questions first, then full analysis |
